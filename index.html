🔬 Siphoning Phenomenon

How Does Water Travel Through Fabric?

Two containers — one filled with water. A fabric wick bridges them. Watch capillary action and gravity work together to transfer water.

🔬 The Science Behind Siphoning

When a fabric wick (like cotton) bridges two containers, water molecules cling to the fibers (adhesion) and to each other (cohesion). This drives water upward against gravity — a process called capillary action. Once the water passes the peak of the wick and flows downward, gravity takes over, pulling it into the lower container. This combined effect is the open capillary siphon.

The 4 Stages of Capillary Siphoning

1

Wetting the Wick

Water molecules adhere to the fabric fibers. Hydrogen bonds between water and cellulose create a strong pull — this is adhesion. The wick becomes saturated as water climbs against gravity.

2

Climbing the Wick (Capillary Action)

Surface tension and adhesion drive water up through the tiny channels between fibers. The smaller the gap, the stronger the capillary force — described by the Lucas-Washburn equation: h ∝ √(γ·r·t / η).

3

Passing the Peak

Once water reaches the highest point of the wick, the column on the far side becomes heavier. Gravity now pulls water downward into the second container. The atmospheric pressure difference sustains the flow.

4

Equilibrium

Flow continues until both containers reach the same water level. At equilibrium, there is no height difference, so the net gravitational force becomes zero and the siphon stops.

📐 Key Physics

h(t) ∝ √(γ · r · t / η)  |  ΔP = ρgh  |  Q = πr⁴ΔP / (8ηL)

Where γ = surface tension, r = pore radius, η = viscosity, ΔP = pressure difference, ρ = water density, h = height, Q = flow rate. Smaller pores → stronger capillary pull. Greater height difference → faster siphon flow.

🌍 What is a Water Footprint?

The water footprint of a food item measures all freshwater used in its entire production process — from growing crops and feeding animals, to processing and packaging. It includes green water (rainfall), blue water (irrigation), and grey water (pollution dilution). This concept was developed by hydrologist Arjen Hoekstra and published in peer-reviewed research on global water use.
